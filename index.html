Why Bridges Matter

Engineering That Connects Humanity

Bridges are more than just structures — they are symbols of human ingenuity, perseverance, and the desire to connect. From the simplest wooden footbridge to the longest suspension spans, every bridge tells a story of overcoming obstacles.

They carry economies, cultures, and dreams across rivers, valleys, and seas. Without bridges, the world as we know it would simply not exist.

Iconic Bridges Around the World

Explore the most breathtaking bridges — each a masterpiece of engineering, design, and human determination.

Suspension USA

Golden Gate Bridge

Completed in 1937, this iconic red-orange suspension bridge spans 1,280m across the Golden Gate strait in San Francisco. It was the longest suspension bridge in the world until 1964 and remains one of the most photographed structures on Earth.

Bascule UK

Tower Bridge

A combined bascule and suspension bridge over the River Thames, completed in 1894. Its twin Gothic towers and distinctive blue color make it London's most recognizable landmark. The bridge can open to let ships pass through.

Arch Italy

Ponte Vecchio

Built in 1345, this medieval stone arch bridge over the Arno River in Florence is famous for the shops built along it. It's the oldest surviving segmented arch bridge in Europe and a symbol of the Renaissance era.

Cable-Stayed USA

Brooklyn Bridge

Opened in 1883, this hybrid cable-stayed/suspension bridge connects Manhattan and Brooklyn. It was the longest bridge in the world at the time and was dubbed the "Eighth Wonder of the World." Over 150,000 people crossed it on opening day.

Cable-Stayed France

Millau Viaduct

The tallest bridge in the world at 343 meters — taller than the Eiffel Tower! Spanning the Tarn Valley in southern France, this cable-stayed bridge was completed in 2004 and is considered one of the greatest engineering achievements of the 21st century.

Suspension Japan

Akashi Kaikyo Bridge

The longest suspension bridge in the world with a main span of 1,991 meters. It connects Kobe to Awaji Island in Japan and was designed to withstand earthquakes up to magnitude 8.5 and typhoons with winds up to 286 km/h.

Types

Bridge Types

Suspension

Main cables hang between towers, with smaller cables supporting the deck. Used for the longest spans.

Arch

Uses a curved structure to transfer weight to abutments at each end. One of the oldest bridge designs.

Cable-Stayed

Cables run directly from towers to the deck in a fan or harp pattern. Modern and efficient design.

Beam

Simple horizontal beams supported at each end. The most basic and common type of bridge worldwide.
